How to Borrow at 4% When Your Bank Charges 7%

The options strategy Wall Street uses daily — and your advisor has never heard of.
 

Last week, a prospect sent me their portfolio. Two brokerage accounts, a 401(k), and an old IRA from a job they left in 2018. On paper, it looked fine — diversified, reasonable fees, decent returns.

The mechanics are straightforward: you simultaneously buy and sell puts and calls at different strikes on a broad index. The result is a defined, known borrowing cost locked in at execution. Think of it as a zero-coupon bond created through the options market — with the OCC (Options Clearing Corporation) guaranteeing settlement.

The fix wasn't complicated. It never is. We moved the bonds into the IRA and repositioned the equities into taxable accounts where they'd benefit from long-term capital gains rates. Same portfolio. Same risk. Same expected return.

"Over a 25-year horizon, the projected tax savings came to $4.5 million. Not from a new strategy — from putting the same pieces on the right squares."

This is what I mean when I say most portfolios aren't broken — they're just badly organized. The investments are fine. The allocation is fine. But the tax drag is eating six figures a decade because nobody asked the simple question: which account should this live in?

The best part? You stay fully invested. Your portfolio keeps compounding. You access the liquidity you need without selling a single share and without triggering a single taxable event. This isn't a hack. It's not a loophole. CME Group published a white paper on it. Cboe has an entire education series dedicated to it. Bloomberg just called the broader strategy a "$1 trillion business." It's been available to institutional investors for decades. The only thing that's changed is that now, you can access it too. If your bank is charging you 6%+ to borrow against your own money, we should talk.
