Your Advisor's Firm Just Got Acquired. Here's What That Means for You.

Private equity bought your advisor's firm. They didn't do it for your benefit.
 

I get this question almost every week: "How do you compete with big banks on lending?" I don't. I make them irrelevant.

Here's what happens after a PE acquisition. The press release says \"nothing changes.\" Then, quietly: fees creep up. Proprietary products appear on the shelf. Your advisor's favorite fund gets replaced by one the new parent company makes money on. The team that knew you leaves. The compliance department doubles. And the culture that made you trust them in the first place? Gone.

This isn't speculation. PE-backed buyers were behind 56.8% of RIA deals over $1 billion last year. Deal activity is on pace to break records again in 2026. The playbook is simple: buy the firm, cut costs, cross-sell products, flip it in 3-5 years. That's not wealth management. That's a leveraged buyout wearing a fiduciary hat.

"\"Independent\" is the most abused word in wealth management. Half the firms calling themselves independent have a PE firm on the cap table with a return target and a timeline. Ask your advisor one question: who owns your firm? If the answer takes more than one sentence, you have your answer."

True independence means no outside owners with a return target. No one telling me what products to use. No pressure to grow at your expense. My incentive is simple: keep you happy — because if you leave, I don't eat.

RIAs now manage 33% of all advisor-managed assets in the US. The independent model is winning — not because of marketing, but because clients are figuring out that alignment matters more than brand names. If your advisor's firm just got acquired — or if you're wondering whether it's next — it might be time for a conversation with someone whose only investor is you.
