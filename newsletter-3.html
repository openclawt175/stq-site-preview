Your Advisor Manages Your Stocks. Who Manages Your Tax Bill?

The most expensive line item in your financial life isn't your advisor's fee. It's the one neither of you is managing.
 

I recently sat down with a prospect earning $450K with a $2.5M portfolio. Great career. Smart guy. His advisor had him in a diversified mix of ETFs — nothing wrong with it on the surface.

Then I looked at where everything was held. His bond fund — generating ordinary income taxed at 37% — was sitting in his taxable brokerage. His growth stocks — which would have qualified for 20% long-term capital gains — were locked inside his traditional IRA, guaranteeing ordinary income tax on every dollar when he withdraws.

His advisor had never looked at his tax return. Not once in six years. He was paying more in avoidable taxes than he was paying in advisory fees. And his advisor had no idea.

"The average high-net-worth investor in California loses 1.5–2% annually to avoidable tax drag. On a $2.5M portfolio, that's $37,000–$50,000 a year. Exposed to one question: where does each investment live?"

The fix took an afternoon. We moved his bonds into the IRA. Repositioned his growth stocks into the taxable account. Added a tax-loss harvesting overlay. Same portfolio. Same risk. Same expected return. Projected savings over 20 years: north of $700K.

This isn't a complex strategy. It's not a new product. It's asset location — putting the right investments in the right accounts based on how they're taxed. And it's the single most overlooked source of alpha in wealth management. Morningstar estimates tax-aware strategies add 0.50%+ annually. Vanguard puts it at 0.50–1.00%. On a multi-million dollar portfolio, compounded over decades, that's generational wealth. Ask your advisor one question: have you ever looked at my tax return? If the answer is no — or worse, if they don't understand why you're asking — you know what to do.
